The Film

A People the Government Refuses to See

Unrecognized follows the descendants of Duwamish people who stayed on their ancestral lands around Puget Sound after 1855 — people who did not relocate to government-designated reservations, and who as a consequence have been systematically denied federal tribal recognition for over a century.

The film examines the human cost of that denial: lost land rights, no access to federal services, cultural erasure — and the fierce, generational effort to make the United States acknowledge what was never given up.

It also turns an unflinching lens on a painful and rarely discussed dynamic: opposition from other recognized tribal nations whose own sovereignty and resources are bound up in the politics of recognition.

History & Context

The Dispersement After 1855

The Point Elliott Treaty was meant to resolve the question of indigenous land in the Pacific Northwest. Instead, it fractured communities — and planted the seed of an injustice that persists to this day.

Understanding the film requires understanding what happened to the Duwamish people in the decades that followed.

January 22, 1855

Point Elliott Treaty Signed

The United States and dozens of Puget Sound tribes sign the treaty at Mukilteo. Duwamish signatories cede millions of acres. In exchange: a reservation — but not on their traditional homeland. Some Duwamish leaders refused.

1855 – 1900s

The Dispersement

The promised Duwamish reservation was never established. Some families relocated to other reservations; others stayed along the Duwamish River and Elliott Bay. The federal government stopped counting them.

1925 – 1970s

Surviving Without Recognition

Duwamish who remained on their ancestral lands built community without federal support. No health services. No land rights. No legal existence as a tribe under U.S. law. Their children grew up in a city built on their homeland.

1979 – 2001

Decades of Federal Petitioning

The Duwamish Tribal Society files for federal acknowledgment. The Bureau of Indian Affairs opens a case that stretches across administrations. A decision finally comes in January 2001 — recognition is granted, then reversed within days by the incoming Bush administration.

Today

Still Unrecognized

The Duwamish people continue to fight for the recognition they have sought for over a century. Their longhouse stands at the mouth of the Duwamish River — on land the city of Seattle acknowledges as their ancestral home, yet refuses to recognize them as a sovereign people.
